# Year 2, Lesson 6: AI Detectives — Ethics & Misinformation 🕵️🌐

## Teacher's Guide

**Course:** AI Concepts Deep Dive (Year 2, Term 5)
**Duration:** 60-75 minutes
**Format:** Live online via Zoom
**Term:** 5 of 8 | **Week:** 6 of 8

---

## 📋 Lesson Overview

### Purpose

This is a **discussion-and-planning** lesson, not a coding lesson — a deliberate change of gear after five weeks of building. It has two big jobs:

1. **Build ethical AI literacy.** Students already understand *how* AI works (rules, data, patterns, chatbots, bias). Now they learn to be sceptical, responsible users: spotting deepfakes and misinformation, protecting privacy, and using AI honestly. This is arguably the most important, most "grown-up" lesson of the term.
2. **Launch the term project.** By the end, every student must leave with a **chosen AI Investigation Report topic** and a clear picture of the report structure. Week 7 is the build workshop; this week is the briefing and planning, and it only works if students walk out knowing exactly what they're investigating.

The energy is different from a code sprint. Success here looks like **rich chat discussion, strong reasoning, and confident topic choices** — not lines of code. Lean into the "detective" framing; it gives sceptical thinking a fun identity.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Explain what deepfakes and AI-generated media are, and why "looking real" is not proof of being real
2. Describe how AI can create misinformation at scale, and name questions to verify a claim (source, cross-check, date, motive)
3. Explain why AI uses data about people, and state one privacy-protecting habit
4. Recite the Responsible AI Citizen code (double-check, give credit, don't deceive, protect privacy)
5. List practical tips for spotting AI content — and know cross-checking the source beats them all
6. Choose an AI Investigation Report topic and describe the report's structure

### Key Success Metrics

- [ ] Every student votes and gives reasons in the "Real or AI?" game
- [ ] Every student leaves with a **chosen investigation topic** posted in chat
- [ ] Students can articulate "it looks real ≠ it is real" in their own words
- [ ] Homework outlines start arriving before Week 7

### Materials Needed

- Computer with internet, Zoom (screen share), Trinket (light use only today)
- **A prepared set of "Real or AI?" examples** — see the box below; prepare these BEFORE class
- The report checklist (in the lesson) ready to screen-share
- This teaching guide open during class
- Class WhatsApp/email for sharing Trinket links

### Preparing the "Real or AI?" examples (do this before class!)

You need **5-8 short examples** for Round 1. You do NOT need real deepfakes — descriptions work perfectly and avoid showing anything upsetting. Good mix:
- A described image with a tell (e.g. "a smiling person holding a mug — but look, the hand has six fingers")
- A too-perfect / generic block of text vs a genuine messy human review
- A dramatic "quote" attributed to a famous person with no source
- One example that is **genuinely impossible to be sure about** — this is the most important one, because it teaches "verify, don't guess"
- Keep every example **age-appropriate and impersonal** — never use real classmates, real tragedies, or anything scary

Have the "answer + the tell" ready for each, so you can reveal and explain.

### Pre-Lesson Preparation (15 minutes before class)

1. **Test Zoom + screen share + chat.** Chat is the main tool today — make sure everyone can post.
2. **Finalise your "Real or AI?" set** and rehearse the reveal for each.
3. **Screen-ready the report checklist** (lesson Part 6) — you'll walk through it live.
4. **Prepare 2-3 example topics** to suggest to students who freeze (video recommender, autocorrect, a game bot).
5. **Have a plan for over-sharing** — if a student types something too personal into the privacy discussion, gently redirect.

### Quick Reference Timeline

```
⏱️  0-8 min   → Hook: deepfakes & "looking real ≠ being real"
```

**Flexible timing:** The two non-negotiables are the **"Real or AI?" game** and the **project briefing + topic choice**. If time runs short, cut or shorten the Trinket checklist code (Part 7) — it is explicitly the optional light touch. Never cut the topic choice; students must leave with a topic.

## 📚 Detailed Teaching Guide

### Part 1-2: Deepfakes & Misinformation (Discussion, ~12 min)

Open with a hook, not a definition:

> "Imagine a video of me — your teacher — saying I'm cancelling all homework forever. It looks exactly like me. It sounds exactly like me. Should you believe it? Today you learn why the answer is: NOT until you check."

Teach deepfakes and misinformation as a *scepticism* skill, not a fear lesson. The single sentence you want every student to repeat back is: **"looking real is not the same as being real."** Put it in the chat and ask them to type it back.

For misinformation, land the **scale** point with the fake-reviews story (Kwame in the lesson): the danger isn't that lies are new — it's that AI makes them cheap, fast, and convincing by the thousand.

**Teaching tips:**
- Keep it **calm and empowering**, not scary. The message is "you have the tools to handle this," not "the internet is terrifying."
- Draw on **prior weeks constantly** — training data (W2), hallucinations (W4), bias (W5). This week ties the whole term together.
- Watch the chat for students who want to share real examples — celebrate good ones, gently steer away from anything involving real named people.

### Part 3-4: Privacy & Responsible AI Citizen (~10 min)

Make privacy **concrete and personal** (but safe): the "would I be okay if this appeared on the class screen?" test is memorable — use it. Do NOT ask students to share actual private information as examples; keep it hypothetical.

Present the **Responsible AI Citizen code** as four rules to carry for life. Tie "double-check" explicitly back to Week 4 hallucinations — this callback cements it. Discuss the "AI-wrote-my-essay" scenario openly: the goal isn't "never use AI," it's **honest, credited, thoughtful** use.

**Teaching tips:**
- Some students will have strong opinions on the "fake video as a joke" question — great. Let a real (respectful) debate happen; it's the heart of the lesson.
- If a student reveals something concerning about their own privacy/data online, follow your safeguarding process privately after class.

### Part 5 + Activity Round 1: Spotting AI + "Real or AI?" Game (~12 min)

Teach the spotting tips quickly (weird hands, gibberish text, too-perfect writing), then **immediately** play Round 1 so they apply it. Run the examples one at a time:

1. Students type **REAL** or **AI** in chat
2. Then type **one reason**
3. You reveal the answer AND the tell

**The reason is the assessment, not the vote.** Praise good reasoning even when the vote is wrong. When you hit the deliberately-impossible example, celebrate anyone who says "I can't tell — I'd verify the source." That's the winning answer of the whole game.

**Teaching tips:**
- Keep it snappy — 6-8 quick examples, ~1 minute each.
- Reinforce after every reveal: the visual tells are getting weaker each year; **cross-checking the source is the skill that lasts.**

### Part 6 + Activity Round 2: PROJECT BRIEFING + Topic Choice (~16 min, the core)

This is the most important stretch of the lesson. Screen-share the **report checklist** and walk through all seven parts slowly. For each section, give a mini-example using a familiar AI (e.g. a video recommender):

- **My AI System:** "YouTube-style recommender — suggests videos."
- **The Data:** "What you watch, like, and skip." (callback W2)
- **How It Decides:** "Learning from examples — it finds patterns in what you watch." (callback W1/W3)
- **What Could Go Wrong:** "It could keep showing only one kind of video — that's a fairness/bias issue." (callback W5)
- **Is It Responsible?:** "Verdict + one fix, e.g. 'let users reset their suggestions.'"
- **My Demo:** "Reuse my Week 1 classifier or Week 4 chatbot."
- **My Verdict:** "2-3 sentences — would I trust it, and why."

Then run **Round 2**: every student posts their chosen topic + one thing they know + one open question. **Circulate through the chat and reply to each** — your main job here is right-sizing topics. Redirect the too-huge ("the whole internet") and the too-tiny, and reassure the stuck with your prepared examples.

**Teaching tips:**
- **Do not let anyone leave without a topic.** If a student is frozen, assign one of the safe defaults (autocorrect, a game opponent) — they can change it later.
- Emphasise the **demo reuse**: students often panic thinking they must build something new. Remind them their W1 classifier / W4 chatbot is perfect.
- Note privately which students have strong vs shaky topics — the shaky ones need a check-in before Week 7.

### Part 7: Light Code + Homework + Wrap-Up (~12 min)

The Trinket "misinformation checker" is a **light, optional** touch — a `list` + `for` loop printing a checklist. If time is tight, demo it on your screen and set it as part of homework rather than doing it live. Its real value is thematic (it reinforces the detective questions), not technical.

Walk through the **homework**: choose topic + draft the five-question outline in comments + name the demo. Stress that this outline **is** the start of their report — good work now makes Week 7 easy.

**Wrap-up script:**

> "You're AI detectives now. You can spot a fake, guard your privacy, and use AI honestly — and you've each chosen an AI to investigate. Next week we build the report. Come with your outline ready. I'm proud of you."

Stay on 2-3 minutes for topic questions.

## 🎓 Assessment & Notes

### During Class, Observe:

**Understanding (discussion quality):**
- [ ] Can explain "looking real ≠ being real" in their own words
- [ ] Gives real reasons (not just guesses) in the "Real or AI?" game
- [ ] Names at least one way to verify a claim (source / cross-check)
- [ ] Can state one Responsible AI Citizen rule and one privacy habit

**Project readiness:**
- [ ] Chose a well-sized investigation topic
- [ ] Can name the data, the decision method, and one risk for their topic
- [ ] Knows which demo program they'll reuse

### Students to Watch

**Need Extra Support:**
- Couldn't choose a topic, or chose one far too broad/narrow — send a suggested topic and a 5-minute check-in before Week 7
- Quiet in discussion — check they understood via a private chat prompt

**Ready for More Challenge:**
- Point them at the ⭐⭐⭐ homework tier (find a real source to cross-check a fact) and suggest a second risk/fairness angle for their report

## ⚠️ Troubleshooting Guide

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Discussion is quiet | Use the numbered ethics prompts one at a time; call on 2-3 students by name; post your own opinion first to model it |
| A student over-shares personal data | Gently redirect ("great example — let's keep it general"); follow up privately if it's a safeguarding concern |
| Student can't pick a topic | Offer a default from your prepared list; reassure they can change it in Week 7 |
| Topics are all too broad | Give the right-sizing rule live: "pick ONE app you actually use and can explain in three sentences" |
| Debate gets heated (e.g. "AI cheating") | Welcome it, keep it respectful, land on "honest + credited use is the goal, not zero use" |
| Time running short | Cut/shorten the Trinket checklist code — it's the optional light touch; protect the game and topic choice |
| A student is anxious about deepfakes/misinformation | Reframe: "you now have the tools most adults don't — that's power, not fear" |

## 📊 Post-Lesson Reflection

**After class, spend 5 minutes noting:**

- **Topic map:** who has a strong topic, who has a shaky one? (Shaky ones get a check-in before Week 7.)
- **Discussion depth:** which ethics ideas landed, which need reinforcing at the Week 8 Summit?
- **Missing topics:** anyone who didn't post a topic — chase before Week 7.
- **Homework watch:** flag students to nudge so outlines arrive in time to build from.

---

## 🔗 Continuity Note for Week 7 (Build the Report)

Week 7 is a **build workshop**. Students should arrive with a chosen topic and a drafted outline (this week's homework). The report follows the seven-part checklist introduced today:

1. My AI System 2. The Data 3. How It Decides 4. What Could Go Wrong 5. Is It Responsible? 6. My Demo (reused W1 classifier or W4 chatbot) 7. My Verdict.

Chase any missing topics/outlines this week so nobody starts Week 7 from scratch. The Week 8 Summit is where reports are presented and the **AI Scholar Badge** is awarded.

## 💭 Remember

**Today's win is judgement, not code.**

Every student should leave able to say two things: "I can tell when to be sceptical of AI" and "I know exactly what AI I'm investigating and how to structure my report." If they believe both, Week 7 builds itself.

**You're growing thoughtful, honest AI citizens — that matters more than any single program.** 🕵️🌍
