# Year 2, Lesson 8: AI Summit Showcase & AI Scholar Badge 🔍🏅

## Teacher's Guide

**Course:** AI Concepts Deep Dive (Year 2, Term 5)
**Duration:** 60-75 minutes
**Format:** Live online via Zoom
**Term:** 5 of 8 | **Week:** 8 of 8

---

## 📋 Lesson Overview

### Purpose

This is the Term 5 finale — a celebration, consolidation, and assessment session, not a new-content lesson. Students present the AI Investigation Report they researched and built over the term, debate a big AI question, take a concepts quiz, reflect, and earn the **AI Scholar Badge**. The goals:

1. **Give every student a moment of pride** — presenting an original investigation of a real AI system to peers is a genuine milestone; make it feel like one
2. **Consolidate the whole term** — the quiz revisits rules vs learning, training vs prediction, features vs labels, causes of bias, how LLMs work, hallucinations, deepfakes, and why to verify AI answers
3. **Build critical, respectful thinkers** — the debate teaches students to reason with evidence and disagree with ideas, not people
4. **Award the AI Scholar Badge** — the emotional peak of the term
5. **Launch Term 6** — send them off excited to *build* their own AI-powered assistant

There is deliberately **very little new code**. The energy is celebratory. Your job is host, debate chair, quiz-master, and badge-awarder.

> **Note on the badge:** Term 5 DOES award a badge — the **AI Scholar Badge**. Make the ceremony a real moment (see Part 6 of the lesson). This is different from Term 1, which awarded none.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Present their AI Investigation clearly (what it is, its data, how it decides, its risks) and run a demo over Zoom
2. Give kind, specific peer feedback ("Two Stars and a Wish")
3. Argue a side of an AI debate with reasons, respectfully
4. Correctly answer concept questions on learning, bias, LLMs, hallucinations, deepfakes, and verification
5. Articulate their biggest "aha" moment about AI

### Key Success Metrics

- [ ] Every student presents (or has their investigation shown) at least briefly
- [ ] Every student posts at least one piece of peer feedback in the chat
- [ ] The class engages with the debate, giving reasons (not just opinions)
- [ ] The class engages with the concepts quiz
- [ ] Every student receives the AI Scholar Badge
- [ ] Students leave knowing what Term 6 holds and feeling proud

### Materials Needed

- Computer with internet, Zoom with screen share enabled for students
- Students' finished AI Investigation Reports (printed) and their demo Trinkets — remind them in advance!
- The quiz questions (in the lesson) — decide whether to screen-share or read aloud
- The debate motion chosen in advance (default: "AI should be allowed to mark homework")
- The AI Scholar Badge (digital certificate/image, or your platform's badge) ready to award
- This teaching guide

### Pre-Lesson Preparation (15 minutes before class)

1. **Send an advance reminder:** "Bring your printed AI Investigation Report AND your demo Trinket — today is the AI Summit!" Ask students to run their demo once before class.
2. **Decide the presentation order** — volunteers first, then gently invite quieter students. Plan it so it flows without awkward silences.
3. **Prepare the quiz delivery** — read-aloud + chat answers works well; have the answer key (in the lesson) open.
4. **Choose and prep the debate** — pick a motion, decide how you'll split the class into FOR/AGAINST, and have the sentence starters ready to paste in chat.
5. **Have the badge ready** — know exactly how you'll award it (screen-share the badge image, read each name, etc.).
6. **Have a backup for unfinished investigations** — anyone without a demo can present the class rule-based classifier/chatbot from earlier weeks and explain it as an example.

## 📚 Detailed Teaching Guide

### Part 1: Welcome + Presentation Pit-Stop (7 minutes)

Open with genuine pride — this is the end of a term that changed how they see AI.

> "Eight weeks ago, AI felt like magic. Now you can explain that it learns from examples, that its data can be biased, that a chatbot predicts the next word and can be confidently wrong. Today you present it, you defend your thinking, and you earn the AI Scholar Badge."

**Presentation pit-stop (no new research):** students run their demo once and mentally tick the presentation-ready checklist (one-sentence summary, the data, how it decides, a risk, report ready, demo runs, short, backup question). Quick fixes only.

#### Teaching Tips

- **"Understanding beats fancy."** Discourage last-minute rebuilding — a short demo that runs beats a complex one that crashes mid-talk.
- **Triage unfinished investigations now.** Anyone without a working demo can present a class example (the Week 3 classifier or Week 4 chatbot) and explain it, or walk through their report verbally.

---

### Part 2: The AI Summit (23 minutes)

The heart of the lesson. Each student follows the 6-step format from the lesson: introduce → what it is → its data → how it decides → a risk → run demo + take one question.

**Run peer feedback in parallel** via the Zoom chat: 🔥 reactions plus "Two Stars and a Wish" (two things liked, one kind suggestion).

#### Teaching Tips

- **Model the first talk yourself** or have a confident volunteer go first, so the format is clear.
- **Coach the feedback.** The first time someone types "it's good", redirect: "Be specific — what exactly did you like about their point?" Reinforce: kind, specific, helpful.
- **Push for the four questions.** If a student only shows the demo, gently prompt: "Great — now in one sentence, how does it *decide*?" These four answers are the term's core learning.
- **Protect nervous students.** Offer options: share screen, OR you run their shared Trinket, OR they just talk through their report. Never force screen-sharing.
- **Celebrate every talk by name.** "Let's get a 🔥 in the chat for Ama!" Every scholar should feel seen.
- **Handle live bugs warmly:** "Ooh, a live bug — real scientists hit surprises in public all the time!"

---

### Part 3: The Great AI Debate (12 minutes)

Run a short, structured debate on one age-appropriate motion. Default: **"AI should be allowed to mark homework."** Alternatives: "AI should never decide who gets a job" or "Schools should teach every pupil how AI works."

Split the class into FOR and AGAINST (assigning sides — including sides students *don't* personally hold — is a deliberate, valuable exercise). Insist on **reasons, not just opinions**, and encourage students to bring in term concepts (bias, training data, hallucinations, fairness, verification).

#### Teaching Tips

- **Chair it firmly and kindly.** Alternate sides, keep each point short, and enforce the golden rule: **challenge the idea, never the person.**
- **Reward evidence.** Loudly praise points that use term vocabulary: "Brilliant — you used 'biased training data' to make your case."
- **Use the sentence starters** (in the lesson) for students who freeze. Paste them in chat.
- **No "winner" needed.** If you take a vote, frame it as "which side argued most convincingly", not "who's right". The goal is thinking, not victory.
- **Watch the clock.** Debates can run long — cap it at 12 minutes so the badge ceremony survives.

#### A simple, fair debate structure (if you want more shape)

If your class enjoys structure, run these mini-rounds:

1. **Opening (2 min):** one speaker per side gives their strongest single reason
2. **Rebuttal (3 min):** the other side responds — "we heard you say X, but…"
3. **Open floor (4 min):** anyone may add a point or question, alternating sides
4. **Closing (2 min):** each side sums up in one sentence
5. **Reflect (1 min):** ask "did anyone's mind shift, even a little?" — changing your view based on a good argument is a *win*, not a loss

Keep a visible tally of the term concepts used (bias, training data, hallucination, verification) — it rewards applying what they learned and keeps the debate anchored to real understanding rather than gut feeling.

---

### Part 4: Term 5 Concepts Quiz (12 minutes)

Run the 12-question quiz from the lesson as a fast, low-stakes chat game. Read each question; students race to answer in the chat; reveal the answer and celebrate. This quiz tests **concepts, not code**.

The quiz deliberately hits every idea of the term: rules vs learning (Q1, Q11), training vs prediction order (Q2), features vs labels (Q3), the cause of bias (Q4, Q10), how LLMs work (Q5), hallucinations (Q6), deepfakes (Q7), verifying AI answers (Q8, Q12), and whether AI truly "understands" (Q9).

#### Teaching Tips

- **First correct answer gets a 🏆, everyone who answers gets a 👏.** Keep it about celebrating understanding, not competition.
- **Linger on Q4 and Q10 (bias).** The core insight — bias comes from the *data*, not a "mean" computer — is the term's most important takeaway. Make sure it lands.
- **Linger on Q12 (verify).** This is the habit you want them to carry for life: an AI answer is a starting point to check, not a final truth.
- **Accept reasonable wording.** Answers are conceptual; "made-up answer" is fine for "hallucination". Reward the understanding, not the exact word.

---

### Part 5: AI Scholar Badge Ceremony + Reflection (10 minutes)

The emotional peak. Award the **AI Scholar Badge** to every student with real ceremony (see Part 6 of the lesson): read each name, the class cheers in chat, and say the AI Scholar pledge together.

Then students reflect in chat: their biggest "aha" moment and the thing that surprised them most.

#### Teaching Tips

- **Make the badge a moment.** Read names slowly, pause for the chat cheer, and say something specific about each student where you can. This is what they'll remember.
- **Frame what the badge means:** not "you can use AI" but "you understand it and use it wisely." That distinction is the whole term.
- **Read several reflections aloud.** Naming students' insights publicly cements the learning going into Term 6.
- **Normalise the range of "ahas".** Different students found different ideas eye-opening — that's the point; together they hold the whole picture.

---

### Part 6: Term 6 Preview + Homework + Wrap-Up (11 minutes)

Reveal the full Year 2 roadmap (Terms 1–5 ✅), then hype Term 6 specifically:

> "This term you learned to see INSIDE AI. Next term — Working with APIs — you stop investigating other people's AI and start building your OWN AI-powered assistant. And because you're an AI Scholar now, you'll build one that's honest and fair."

Walk through the homework: the "AI Scholar Certificate" program (Option A) or the "most important thing I learned" written piece (Option B). Point at the solution code as an example of a small celebratory program.

Close with BrightByte's final word and genuine celebration.

#### Teaching Tips

- **The Term 6 "you'll BUILD one" hook is the motivation** — emphasise that they move from investigator to maker.
- **End on a high.** Stay on the call a few minutes for students who want to keep chatting or show one more thing.

## 🎓 Assessment & Notes

### End-of-Term Progress Check

Use today to finalise your term picture of each student:

**Confident / ready for Term 6:**
- Presented a clear investigation covering all four questions (what/data/decides/risk); answered concept-quiz questions across multiple topics; reasoned well in the debate

**Needs consolidation over the break:**
- Investigation was incomplete or only described the demo; struggled with the core concepts (especially the cause of bias, and that LLMs predict the next word / can hallucinate)
- Send these students the relevant Week 2 (features/labels), Week 5 (bias), and Week 6 (LLMs/misinformation) lesson links and a friendly nudge

### Observe:
- [ ] Presented or had their investigation shown
- [ ] Gave at least one specific piece of peer feedback
- [ ] Contributed a reasoned point to the debate
- [ ] Engaged with the concepts quiz

## ⚠️ Troubleshooting Guide

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Student's demo crashes / investigation unfinished | Have them present a class example (Week 3 classifier / Week 4 chatbot) and explain it, or talk through their report verbally |
| Student refuses to share screen | Offer alternatives: you run their shared Trinket, or they describe their investigation and one risk verbally |
| Awkward silence, no presentation volunteers | Model the format yourself first, or call a confident student you prepped in advance |
| Feedback chat goes quiet | Prompt specifically: "Drop a 🔥 and one thing you liked about Kofi's point on training data!" |
| Debate turns personal or heated | Reset the golden rule: "We challenge the idea, not the person." Redirect to a sentence starter |
| Debate is one-sided / quiet | Assign sides explicitly; give the quiet side a strong starter reason to build on |
| Quiz too easy / too hard | Skip ahead or slow down; for fast classes add a bonus "explain WHY" on Q4, Q10, and Q12 |
| Large class, not enough time for all talks | Two rounds, cap at 90 seconds each, or use breakout rooms with a spokesperson |
| Student forgot report/Trinket | Let them present a class example and explain it as if investigating it — still a valid experience |

## 📊 Post-Lesson Reflection

**After class, spend 5 minutes noting:**

- **Term-level wins:** which students grew most in their *thinking* about AI? Who became genuinely critical and curious?
- **Consolidation list:** who needs support before Term 6, and on what specifically (bias? how LLMs work? verifying answers?)
- **What worked this term:** which analogies (plantains, features/labels, autocomplete-for-LLMs) and activities landed best — carry them forward
- **Summit logistics:** did talks, debate, quiz, and ceremony fit the time? Adjust the balance for future showcase weeks

## 💭 Remember

**Today is about pride and perspective, not perfection.**

This term did something unusual: it made powerful, mysterious technology *understandable* to 11–13 year olds. Students who leave feeling "I know how AI works, I can question it, and I'll use it wisely" walk into a world full of AI as thoughtful citizens rather than passive users. The AI Scholar Badge marks exactly that. Make sure every student knows what it really means — and that in Term 6, they get to build one of these systems themselves.
