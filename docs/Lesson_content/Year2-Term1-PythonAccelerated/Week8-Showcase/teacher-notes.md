# Year 2, Lesson 8: Calculator Showcase & Celebration! 🎉🧮

## Teacher's Guide

**Course:** Python Accelerated (Year 2, Term 1)
**Duration:** 60-75 minutes
**Format:** Live online via Zoom
**Term:** 1 of 8 | **Week:** 8 of 8

---

## 📋 Lesson Overview

### Purpose

This is the Term 1 finale — a celebration and consolidation session, not a new-content lesson. Students demo the Smart Calculator they built over Weeks 6-7, give each other feedback, play a term quiz, and reflect. The goals:

1. **Give every student a moment of pride** — presenting your own working program to peers is a milestone; make it feel like an achievement
2. **Consolidate the whole term** — the quiz revisits data types, maths operators, f-strings, if/elif/else, validation, and the string-vs-number trap in one fun pass
3. **Build a supportive maker culture** — kind, specific peer feedback teaches students to celebrate and constructively critique code
4. **Launch Term 2** — send them off excited about the Arcade Game Collection and their first Year 2 badge

There is deliberately **very little new code**. The energy is celebratory. Your job is host, cheerleader, and quiz-master.

> **Note on badges:** Term 1 awards NO badge (the first Year 2 badge, the Python Pro Badge, comes at the end of Term 2). Do not promise a Term 1 badge — instead tease the Term 2 badge as something to look forward to.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Demo their own program confidently over Zoom (share screen, run it, explain a feature)
2. Give kind, specific peer feedback ("Two Stars and a Wish")
3. Correctly answer term-review questions on types, operators, f-strings, logic, and validation
4. Articulate their proudest moment and one challenge they overcame

### Key Success Metrics

- [ ] Every student runs their calculator live (or has it shown) at least briefly
- [ ] Every student posts at least one piece of peer feedback in the chat
- [ ] The class engages with the quiz (racing answers in chat)
- [ ] Students leave knowing what Term 2 holds and feeling proud

### Materials Needed

- Computer with internet, Zoom with screen share enabled for students
- Students' finished `Y2-T1-W7-CalculatorDeluxe` Trinkets (remind them in advance!)
- The quiz questions (in the lesson) — decide whether to screen-share or read aloud
- This teaching guide
- Class WhatsApp group/email for sharing links and celebration

### Pre-Lesson Preparation (15 minutes before class)

1. **Send an advance reminder:** "Bring your finished calculator — today you demo it!" Ask students to run it once before class.
2. **Decide the demo order** — volunteers first, then gently invite quieter students. Have a plan so it flows without awkward silences.
3. **Prepare the quiz delivery** — read-aloud + chat answers works well; have the answer key (in the lesson) open.
4. **Have a backup for broken calculators** — anyone whose program crashes can demo the polished solution or do a "polish pit-stop" with you live.
5. **Prepare the Term 2 hype** — the Arcade Game Collection and Python Pro Badge are your closing motivation.
6. **Optional:** prepare a simple "Term 1 Complete" certificate (the lesson's solution code prints one) to share or award.

## 🎯 Lesson Flow (60-75 Minutes)

### Quick Reference Timeline

```
⏱️  0-8 min   → Welcome + BrightByte's proud speech + polish pit-stop
⏱️  8-35 min  → The Grand Showcase (student demos + peer feedback in chat)
⏱️ 35-50 min  → Term 1 Quiz Game
⏱️ 50-60 min  → Reflection (proudest moment / hardest thing)
⏱️ 60-75 min  → Term 2 preview + homework + celebration wrap-up
```

**Flexible timing:** Demo time scales with class size. For a large class, cap demos at ~90 seconds each or do them in two rounds. Protect the reflection and Term 2 preview — they're the emotional payoff.

## 📚 Detailed Teaching Guide

### Part 1: Welcome + Polish Pit-Stop (8 minutes)

Open with genuine pride — this is the end of their first Year 2 term.

> "Eight weeks ago you were shaking rust off `print()`. Today you have a REAL program that doesn't crash even when someone types 'banana' into it. That's not beginner work. Today we celebrate, we show off, and we soak it in."

**Polish pit-stop (no new features):** students run their calculator once and tick the demo-ready checklist (runs, menu clear, maths correct, formatted, divide-by-zero guarded, bad-input guarded, quits cleanly). Quick fixes only.

#### Teaching Tips

- **"Working beats fancy."** Discourage last-minute feature-adding — a calculator that works beats one that breaks mid-demo.
- **Triage broken calculators now.** If someone's program crashes, pair with them quietly during the showcase or let them demo the class solution.

---

### Part 2: The Grand Showcase (27 minutes)

The heart of the lesson. Each student follows the 5-step demo format from the lesson: introduce → run live → two calculations → explain one proud feature → take one question.

**Run peer feedback in parallel** via the Zoom chat: 🔥 reactions plus "Two Stars and a Wish" (two things liked, one kind suggestion).

#### Teaching Tips

- **Model the first demo yourself** or have a confident volunteer go first, so the format is clear.
- **Coach the feedback.** The first time someone types "it's good", redirect: "Be specific — what exactly did you like?" Reinforce the golden rule: kind, specific, helpful.
- **Protect nervous students.** Offer options: share screen, OR you run their shared Trinket link, OR they just describe one feature. Never force screen-sharing.
- **Celebrate every demo by name.** "Let's get a 🔥 in the chat for Ama!" Every student should feel seen.
- **Handle live bugs warmly:** "Ooh, a live bug — real programmers debug in public all the time!" Model calm debugging.

---

### Part 3: Term 1 Quiz Game (15 minutes)

Run the 12-question quiz from the lesson as a fast, low-stakes chat game. Read each question; students race to answer in the chat; reveal the answer and celebrate.

The quiz deliberately hits every week: `input()` returns a string (Q1, Q12), `int()`/`float()` (Q2, Q3), `//` `%` `**` `/` (Q4-7), f-strings/`:.2f` (Q8), if/elif/else (Q9), boolean logic (Q10), `.isdigit()` (Q11), and the string-vs-number trap (Q12).

#### Teaching Tips

- **First correct answer gets a 🏆, everyone who answers gets a 👏.** Keep it about celebrating knowledge, not competition.
- **Linger on Q12 (the classic trap).** `input()` stores `"12"` (string), so `age == 12` is `False`. This is THE term's big insight — make sure it lands.
- **Adjust pace to the class.** If they're flying, speed up; if they're unsure, pause and re-teach briefly — this is your last diagnostic of the term.

---

### Part 4: Reflection (10 minutes)

Students share in chat: proudest moment, and one thing they found hard (and how they got through it).

> "Reading each other's reflections reminds us: everyone found something hard, and everyone got through it. That's what coders do."

#### Teaching Tips

- **Read several aloud.** Naming students' wins publicly cements confidence going into Term 2.
- **Normalise struggle.** When students admit something was hard, affirm it: "The bug that took you an hour taught you more than the code that worked first try."

---

### Part 5: Term 2 Preview + Homework + Wrap-Up (15 minutes)

Reveal the full Year 2 roadmap (Term 1 ✅), then hype Term 2 specifically:

> "Next term: Loops & Logic Mastery. You'll build an Arcade Game Collection — real games you can play and share. And for the FIRST time in Year 2, you'll earn a badge: the Python Pro Badge!"

Walk through the homework: the "Term 1 Trophy" certificate program (Option A) or the family demo (Option B). Point at the solution code as an example of the certificate.

Close with BrightByte's final word and genuine celebration.

#### Teaching Tips

- **The badge tease is motivation** — Term 1 has none, so frame the Term 2 badge as the reward ahead.
- **End on a high.** Stay on the call a few minutes for students who want to keep chatting or show one more thing.

## ⚠️ Troubleshooting Guide

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Student's calculator crashes / is unfinished | Do a quick polish with them during others' demos, or let them present the class solution and explain it |
| Student refuses to share screen | Offer alternatives: you run their shared link, or they describe one feature verbally |
| Awkward silence, no demo volunteers | Go first yourself, or call a confident student you prepped in advance |
| Feedback chat goes quiet | Prompt specifically: "Drop a 🔥 and one thing you liked about Kofi's menu!" |
| Quiz too easy / too hard | Skip ahead or slow down; add a bonus "explain WHY" for fast classes |
| Large class, not enough time for all demos | Two rounds, cap at 90 seconds each, or group demos into breakout rooms with a spokesperson |
| Student forgot their Trinket | Have them open the class solution and walk through it as if it were theirs — still a valid demo experience |

## 📊 Post-Lesson Reflection

**After class, spend 5 minutes noting:**

- **Term-level wins:** which students grew the most? Who found their confidence?
- **Consolidation list:** who needs support before Term 2, and on what specifically (types? logic? validation?)
- **What worked this term:** which analogies, activities, and projects landed best — carry them into Term 2
- **Showcase logistics:** did demos fit the time? Adjust the format for future showcase weeks (this repeats every term)
