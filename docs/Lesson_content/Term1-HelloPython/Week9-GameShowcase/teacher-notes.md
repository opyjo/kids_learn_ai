## 📋 Lesson Overview

### Purpose

This is the culminating celebration of Term 1. Students present their completed Mad Libs games to the class in a live demo format — running the game with a classmate entering words while the audience shouts suggestions. The showcase format is deliberately interactive: the audience IS part of the game, which reduces presentation anxiety and guarantees laughs. The primary goals are (1) give every student a genuine moment to shine, (2) celebrate individual and collective achievement, (3) build confidence through low-stakes public presentation, and (4) generate curiosity for Term 2. This lesson contains no new Python content — it is about celebration, reflection, and transition.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will have:

1. Presented their completed Mad Libs game to peers in a live demo
2. Reflected on their Term 1 learning journey
3. Celebrated their own and classmates' achievements
4. Understood what if/else will unlock in Term 2
5. Left feeling proud, confident, and genuinely excited for what's next

### Prerequisites

- Students should have completed their Mad Libs game (Week 8 homework)
- All games should be accessible via Trinket before class
- Students who didn't complete: see Differentiation section — every student presents something

### Materials Needed

- Computer with internet access (for displaying student games)
- Projector/screen share capability
- Certificates (printed or digital)
- Awards/recognition materials (optional)
- Timer for presentations
- Celebration items (optional: stickers, treats, class photo)
- Backup plan for technical issues

### Pre-Lesson Preparation

1. **Test all student Trinket links** before class — nothing worse than a showcase delay due to a broken link
2. **Prepare presentation order** — mix skill levels, volunteers first; save a strong one for the end
3. **Print certificates** — personalised for each student
4. **Decide awards** — review all games and choose recipients before class
5. **Prepare Term 2 preview** — the if/else "choose a story" snippet, ready to type live
6. **Prepare updated skills checklist** — confirm it covers all 14 items including input() and int()
7. **Check your own energy** — this is a celebration; your enthusiasm sets the tone

## 📚 Detailed Teaching Guide

### Part 1: Welcome & Energy Setting (2 minutes)

#### Goals

- Create an immediately celebratory atmosphere
- Set positive, supportive tone for presentations
- Reduce nervousness through collective excitement

#### Script/Talking Points

> "Welcome to the Week 9 Game Showcase! 🎉
>
> Nine weeks ago, you had never written a line of Python. Today you're showing off interactive games you built from scratch. That is incredible.
>
> A few quick rules for today:
>
> - When someone's running their game, YOUR job is to give them the SILLIEST words you can think of
> - After every reveal, we react — if it's funny, laugh! That's the reward
> - After every presentation, we clap and say something kind
> - Everyone gets the same applause — we're here to celebrate EVERYONE
>
> Is everyone ready to laugh a lot and be proud of what this class built? Let's go!"

**Get an enthusiastic response.**

---

### Part 2: Final Preparations (8 minutes)

#### Goals

- Give students last chance to fix issues
- Reduce presentation anxiety through preparation
- Handle any technical problems before showcase begins

#### Setup

> "Eight minutes to:
>
> 1. Test your game ONE final time
> 2. Practise your 1-sentence intro out loud
> 3. Confirm your player is ready
>
> If you have any issues, raise your hand NOW."

#### Teacher Actions During Prep

1. **Circulate quickly** — check each student's readiness
2. **Identify broken links** — open Trinket yourself if a student can't
3. **Quick fixes only** — no major debugging now; focus on getting something runnable
4. **Reassure nervous students** — "Your audience is going to give you the worst words and love every second"
5. **Finalise order** — confirm who goes first (should be enthusiastic and confident)

#### Handling Non-Complete Games

If a student didn't finish:

- NEVER exclude them from presenting
- "Show us what you have — even if it's just the welcome screen and 3 inputs"
- "Tell us about the story you PLANNED to build"
- Focus on what they LEARNED, not what they didn't finish
- Script: "Even though [name]'s game isn't quite complete, look at what they did build..."

#### 2-Minute Warning

> "Two minutes! Know your intro. Know who's typing for you. One deep breath. You've got this."

---

### Part 3: Showcase Presentations (30 minutes)

#### Goals

- Every student presents their game
- Audience actively participates (shouting words) — keeps energy high throughout
- Maintain celebratory atmosphere for full 30 minutes

#### The Mad Libs Showcase Format

This showcase is different from a standard project demo because the **audience participates in the game**. This is key — it eliminates the awkward silence during demos.

**Format for each presentation (~2–3 minutes):**

1. **Intro (20–30 sec):** Student gives their 1-sentence description
2. **Input phase (30–60 sec):** Student's player types words; class shouts suggestions enthusiastically
3. **The Reveal (20–30 sec):** Story appears; class reacts
4. **Reflection (20 sec):** "One thing I'm proud of..." or "One thing I learned..."
5. **Applause (10 sec):** Led by teacher; specific compliment

#### Time Management

| Class Size | Time Per Presentation | Strategy |
|------------|----------------------|----------|
| 10–12 | 2.5–3 min | Full format |
| 13–18 | 2 min | Streamlined |
| 19–25 | 1.5 min | Quick showcase |
| 25+ | Consider small groups | Split into two sessions |

**Use a timer.** Gently signal at 1 minute and again at 30 seconds remaining.

#### Leading the Audience

During the input phase, actively facilitate crowd participation:

> "Okay — [student's player] needs an animal. CLASS — what animal should they type?"

Let the class shout. Accept the funniest/weirdest answer. Type it. Move to next input.

This keeps energy high and removes the awkward silence of watching someone type.

#### After Every Presentation

Always lead applause AND give a specific, genuine compliment:

- "I loved the prompt 'Enter a word that describes your teacher' — inspired!"
- "The space theme was perfect for your style of humour"
- "That reveal was genuinely one of the funniest things I've seen in a programming class"
- "The way you used int() to calculate the time — that's Lesson 7 bonus challenge right there!"

#### Handling Technical Issues

| Issue | Quick Solution |
|-------|---------------|
| Trinket won't load | Try a different browser tab; have student describe the game while fixing |
| Code has a last-minute bug | "This is actually a great teachable moment — what does everyone think the bug is?" |
| Student freezes | "Start with: 'My game is about...'" — give them the sentence starter |
| Player types something that breaks the story grammar | That's fine! Laugh and move on |
| Audio/display issues | Read the story output aloud for the class |

#### Presentation Order Strategies

**Recommended (Option 3: Strategic):**

- First presenter: confident, enthusiastic student — sets the tone
- Mix skill levels throughout
- Spread the laughs — alternate between very funny and less funny games
- Save a strong, polished game for the finale

**Lead a proper class cheer.**

### Part 5: Term 1 Reflection (5 minutes)

#### Goals

- Help students recognise the scope of what they learned
- Connect the skills to real-world programming
- Create a genuine sense of achievement

#### Skills Walk-Through (2 minutes)

> "Let's look at everything you learned in 9 weeks."

**Display the week-by-week table from the lesson briefly.**

> "Raise your hand if 9 weeks ago you had NEVER written a line of Python."

[Most hands up]

> "Now raise your hand if you're a Python programmer who has built and presented a complete interactive game."

[All hands up]

> "That is nine weeks of work. That's real."

> "In the lesson, there's a checklist of 14 skills. Take 2 minutes and check off everything you can do now. Be honest — if you're not confident about something, circle it as something to review."

**Let students self-assess silently.**

> "How many got 10 or more? Raise your hand. 12 or more? 14?"

Acknowledge that completing 14/14 is genuinely impressive; completing 10/14 is still a strong Term 1 foundation.

#### Quick Reflection Share (1 minute)

> "One person — what's your proudest moment from Term 1?"

Hear 2–3 responses. Affirm each one genuinely.

### Part 6: Term 2 Preview (5 minutes)

#### Goals

- End with genuine curiosity and excitement
- Show — don't just tell — what if/else unlocks
- Leave students wanting to come back

#### The If/Else Teaser

> "Your Mad Libs game always tells the same story — just with different words. But what if the story could actually CHANGE based on what the player chooses?"

**Type this live on the projector:**

```python
choice = input("Do you want a scary or funny story? ")
# What if the story CHANGED based on the answer?
# That's Term 2...
```

> "Right now, you can't do anything with that 'choice' variable. But in Term 2, you'll learn if/else — and your program can make DECISIONS."

**Type the full snippet:**

```python
mood = input("Are you feeling brave or silly today? ")
if mood == "brave":
    print("A knight rode into battle on a very serious horse...")
else:
    print("A penguin moonwalked into the supermarket...")
```

**Run it. Type "brave". Run it again. Type "silly".**

> "Same program. Completely different story. That's if/else. And that's just the BEGINNING of Term 2."

#### Term 2 Projects Tease

> "In Term 2, you might build:
> - A quiz game that keeps score with if/else
> - A choose-your-own-adventure story with branching paths
> - A number guessing game
>
> If you thought Mad Libs was fun, Term 2 is going to blow your mind."

## 🎯 Assessment & Differentiation

### Assessment Focus

This lesson is about **celebration, not evaluation**. However, note:

**Observation Points:**

- Ability to explain their game to an audience
- Confidence level during presentation
- Peer support and audience behaviour
- Engagement during others' presentations
- Which concepts are most comfortable / least comfortable

**Use for Term 2 Planning:**

- Students who didn't use f-strings confidently: review in Term 2 Week 1
- Students who rushed the design phase: scaffold project planning in Term 2
- Students who attempted int() bonus: ready for early Term 2 challenges

### Differentiation Strategies

#### For Students Who Didn't Complete Their Game

**NEVER exclude a student from presenting.**

Options:

1. Present the welcome screen + as many inputs as they built
2. Describe their full DESIGN (the story on paper) even if not coded
3. "Co-present" with a classmate who can fill in the game
4. Focus the reflection on what they LEARNED rather than what they built

**Script:**

> "Even though [name]'s game isn't quite finished, look at the design they planned out — and notice they got the welcome screen and 4 inputs working. Tell us about the story you were building, [name]..."

#### For Nervous Presenters

- Offer to stand beside them during the presentation
- Allow seated presentation with screen share instead of coming to front
- Reduce requirement: "Just run the game — you don't need to explain anything"
- Present earlier (before anticipation anxiety builds)

#### For Students Who Went Above and Beyond

- Give them the "Above & Beyond" award and call out specifically what they did
- Ask them to briefly explain their int() use or second story to the class
- Consider having them go last as a strong finale

## ⚠️ Common Challenges & Solutions

### Time Management

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| Presentations running over | Strict timer; lead applause as signal to wrap |
| Technical delays eating time | Describe game while fixing; quick 30-second buffer rule |
| Very large class | Small-group showcases running simultaneously; teacher circulates |

### Presentation Issues

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| Student freezes | "Start with: 'My game is about...'" — give the sentence starter |
| Code doesn't run | "Describe what it SHOULD do — you know your game perfectly" |
| Story not funny | YOU laugh and lead applause enthusiastically anyway |
| Audience too rowdy shouting words | "I'm picking ONE suggestion — make it a good one!" |

### Emotional Situations

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| Student upset about incomplete game | Private reassurance; "What you have is real and impressive" |
| Student disappointed about no individual award | "Everyone who presented is in the awards ceremony — that IS the award" |
| Overwhelmed by emotion (positive or negative) | Acknowledge it; it's normal at the end of a big journey |
