**Course:** Term 8: AI Explorer

## 📋 Lesson Overview

### Purpose

This lesson explores AI ethics: fairness, privacy, mistakes, and responsible use. This is a discussion-based lesson that helps students think critically about AI and become responsible AI users. It's important for students to understand not just how AI works, but how to use it ethically.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this lesson, students will be able to:

1. Understand what "fair AI" means
2. Recognize why privacy matters with AI
3. Know what to do when AI makes mistakes
4. Think critically about AI use
5. Use AI responsibly

### Materials Needed

- Computer with internet access (for each student)
- Projector/screen share for discussions
- Whiteboard for recording thoughts
- Optional: Discussion prompts

### Pre-Lesson Preparation

1. **Review AI ethics topics** — Understand fairness, privacy, mistakes
2. **Prepare discussion prompts** — Questions to guide conversation
3. **Think about age-appropriate examples** — Relatable scenarios
4. **Prepare facilitation strategies** — How to guide discussions

## 📚 Detailed Teaching Guide

### Part 1: Review & Hook (5 minutes)

#### Goals

- Review AI concepts
- Introduce ethics
- Build engagement

#### Script/Talking Points

> "Welcome back! You've learned how AI works. But today, we're going to talk about something SUPER important—using AI RESPONSIBLY. AI is powerful, but it's important to think about fairness, privacy, and what to do when AI makes mistakes."

**Quick Review:**

> "Who can remind us: how does AI use data?"

Wait for: stores in lists, processes with loops

> "Perfect! But today we're thinking about: Is AI fair? Is it private? What if it makes mistakes?"

#### Teaching Tips

- Set thoughtful tone
- Emphasize importance
- Encourage participation

---

### Part 2: AI Fairness Discussion (15 minutes)

#### Goals

- Explore fairness concepts
- Discuss real examples
- Encourage critical thinking

#### Discussion: What is Fair?

> "Let's talk about fairness. What does 'fair' mean?"

Let students answer.

> "Now, think about AI. Is AI always fair?"

**Scenario 1: AI Recommendations**

> "Imagine an AI recommends videos. Is it fair if it only recommends certain videos to certain people?"

Guide discussion:
- Should everyone see the same?
- What if it's personalized?
- How can we make it fair?

**Scenario 2: AI in Games**

> "What if an AI makes a game harder for some players? Is that fair?"

Guide discussion:
- Should difficulty be the same?
- What if it's based on skill?
- How do we balance?

#### Teaching Tips

- Encourage all students to participate
- No wrong answers—explore ideas
- Guide toward thoughtful conclusions
- Use whiteboard to record thoughts

---

### Part 3: Privacy Discussion (15 minutes)

#### Goals

- Explore privacy concepts
- Discuss data use
- Think about protection

#### Discussion: Privacy and Data

> "Let's talk about privacy. What data does AI use about you?"

Let students brainstorm.

**Scenario: Voice Assistant**

> "A voice assistant remembers what you say. Is this okay?"

Guide discussion:
- What data is stored?
- Who can access it?
- How can you protect privacy?

**Ways to Protect Privacy:**

> "How can we protect our privacy with AI?"

Discuss:
- Be careful what you share
- Understand data use
- Use privacy settings
- Ask questions

#### Teaching Tips

- Make it relatable
- Use real examples
- Encourage questions
- Provide practical tips

---

### Part 4: Mistakes & Responsibility (15 minutes)

#### Goals

- Discuss AI mistakes
- Explore responsible use
- Think about solutions

#### Discussion: When AI Makes Mistakes

> "AI can make mistakes. What should we do?"

**Scenario: Wrong Answer**

> "An AI gives you wrong information. What do you do?"

Guide discussion:
- Don't trust blindly
- Check important info
- Report mistakes
- Use AI as a tool

**Responsible AI Use:**

> "How can we use AI responsibly?"

Discuss:
- Think critically
- Protect privacy
- Be fair
- Report problems
- Keep learning

#### Teaching Tips

- Encourage critical thinking
- Provide practical guidance
- Make it actionable
- Build responsibility

---

### Part 5: Wrap-up & Homework (7 minutes)

#### Review Key Points

> "What did we learn today?"

Quick review:
1. "What is fair AI?" → Treats everyone equally
2. "Why is privacy important?" → Protects personal information
3. "What do we do with mistakes?" → Don't trust blindly, check info
4. "How do we use AI responsibly?" → Think critically, be fair, protect privacy

#### Introduce Homework

> "For homework, write a reflection about AI ethics. Think about fairness, privacy, mistakes, and responsibility!"

---

### Part 6: Q&A Buffer (3 minutes)

Use this time for questions and clarifications.

## 🤖 AI Activity: AI Court Case Role-Play (5-10 minutes)

### What to Do

1. **Introduction** (1 min)
   > "You've learned about AI ethics. But what if we had to decide: Should AI be allowed to make important decisions?"

2. **Court Case Role-Play** (4-5 min)
   - Set up: "We're having a court case - should AI be allowed to [drive cars / diagnose diseases / grade tests]?"
   - Divide students: Some are "for AI", some are "against AI"
   - Have each side present arguments: "Why should/shouldn't AI do this?"
   - Discuss: "What are the risks? What are the benefits?"

3. **Discussion** (2-3 min)
   - "What did you learn from the court case?"
   - "What are the risks of AI making decisions?"
   - "What are the benefits?"

4. **Connection** (1 min)
   > "These are real questions people are debating right now! Should AI drive cars? Should AI diagnose diseases? By thinking about these questions, you're learning to be a responsible AI user and creator!"

### Discussion Questions

- "Should AI be allowed to make important decisions?"
- "What are the risks? What are the benefits?"
- "How can we make sure AI is used responsibly?"

### Teaching Tips

- Make it fun and engaging - this is role-play!
- Encourage all opinions - there's no "right" answer
- Keep it age-appropriate - focus on fairness and safety
- Use concrete examples: "Should AI drive cars? Why or why not?"
- Emphasize critical thinking: "These are real questions people are asking!"

### Alternative Activity (If Short on Time)

- Quick discussion: "Should AI be allowed to [drive cars / diagnose diseases]?"
- Connect: "These are real questions people are debating!"
- Emphasize: "Thinking about ethics is part of being a responsible programmer!"

## 🎯 Assessment & Differentiation

### Formative Assessment

**During class, observe:**

- [ ] Does student participate in discussions?
- [ ] Does student show understanding of fairness?
- [ ] Does student understand privacy concerns?
- [ ] Does student think critically?
- [ ] Does student show responsibility?

### Homework Assessment: "AI Ethics Reflection"

| Criteria              | Exceeds (3)                    | Meets (2)                  | Developing (1)           |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| **Fairness**          | Thoughtful, detailed response  | Basic understanding        | Unclear or missing       |
| **Privacy**           | Thoughtful, detailed response  | Basic understanding        | Unclear or missing       |
| **Mistakes**          | Thoughtful, detailed response  | Basic understanding        | Unclear or missing       |
| **Responsibility**    | Thoughtful, detailed response  | Basic understanding        | Unclear or missing       |
| **Reflection quality** | Deep, personal reflection      | Basic reflection           | Surface level           |

### Differentiation Strategies

#### For Students Who Need More Support

- Provide discussion prompts
- Use simpler examples
- Focus on one topic at a time
- Pair with peer
- Reduce to 2-3 questions

#### For Advanced Students

- Challenge with complex scenarios
- Ask deeper questions
- Have them lead discussions
- Connect to real-world examples

## ⚠️ Common Challenges & Solutions

### Discussion Challenges

| Problem                  | Signs                          | Solution                                    |
| ------------------------ | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------- |
| Students not participating | Quiet, not engaging           | Use small groups, provide prompts           |
| Off-topic discussions   | Getting distracted             | Gently redirect, use examples              |
| Overwhelmed by concepts  | Too abstract                   | Use concrete, relatable examples           |
